Abstract

Particle-in-cell simulations are needed to model complex scientific problems, such as modelling plasma behaviour to determine how it can be controlled in nuclear fusion reactors. Parallel processing is used because it can process complex equations more efficiently. This performance difference was determined using a simple recursive binary search to compare sequential and parallel processing methods, where varying sizes of arrays were used to measure the average time taken to complete each stage of the search. Another finding was that the current costs of hardware, software and energy consumption requirements were more cost-effective for parallel processing compared to quantum parallelism, which would be more accessible to the average researcher. Overall, the evidence suggests that parallel processing is the most suitable and efficient method for particle-in-cell simulations.

Introduction

Parallel processing is essential for efficiently solving computationally intensive problems across all disciplines of sciences. In modern physics, it allows black holes to be modelled to test theories like Einstein's general relativity without performing experiments that are not yet possible. It is also important in Particle-in-Cell (PIC) simulations which help control high-temperature plasma in nuclear fusion reactors to keep experiments safe. This article will discuss what PIC simulations are, why they are needed followed by what parallel processing is, how it compares to other methods and summarises this in a conclusion.

Analysis

Particle-in-Cell (PIC) simulations and their importance:
PIC simulations "are routinely used as effective tools to understand kinetic phenomena in laboratory, astrophysical and fusion-relevant plasmas" (Higginson et al., 2019), helping scientists control high-temperature plasmas in nuclear fusion reactors. Controlling this prevents damage to the reactor and avoids plasma cooling, which would end the reaction (Goddijn, 2021).This would cause disruptions in energy supply and significant maintenance expenses. Fusion has long-term benefits such as promising to be a safe, low carbon and sustainable energy source (UKAEA, n.d.), offering advantages over non-renewable fossil fuels which are harmful to the environment. To do this, PIC simulations use parallel processing to compute large volumes of data and calculations simultaneously.

Comparison of parallel and sequential processing methods:
Parallel processing executes multiple streams of data concurrently, whereas sequential processing executes tasks one at a time and must be completed before moving on to the next (Lenovo, 2025). Parallel is preferred as it performs calculations simultaneously producing real-time results efficiently. It is evident that the 8-thread search can handle more data and complete tasks faster than sequential processing. PIC simulations involve far more instructions than this example, making parallel processing with multiple threads more suitable. However, there are some drawbacks. For example, when "modelling the plasma as cold, the noise is high" (Grigoryev et al., 2002), meaning large amounts of irrelevant data can be produced, hiding important underlying patterns. Furthermore, "the computational requirements can be very high and they drastically increase with increasing plasma density and size of the calculation domain" (Wünderlich et al., 2018), potentially increasing computational time and reducing the availability of real-time data. These drawbacks suggest that parallel processing is not always suitable for real-time data. Another obstacle is limited software compatibility, which may require more spending to optimise software or purchase specialised hardware.

Comparison of parallel processing and quantum parallelism methods:
An alternative approach that could be used is quantum parallelism, which is "the capability to execute numerous operations concurrently by exploiting quantum physics" (Wu et al., 2025). This method is able to utilise quantum technologies "to accelerate simulations or a part of the plasma simulations" (Hedge et al., 2025), which is particularly useful due to the computational demands of PIC simulations. This is made possible by the speed at which it can compute problems and the scale of data it can take in, "problems that grow in memory requirements, …can be solved with relatively small quantum computers" (QuEra, 2021).

However, with these benefits, there are also disadvantages relating to hardware requirements. An example of this is cost and complexity, as "super low temperatures are required for the quantum computers to work effectively" (Tencent Cloud, 2025), which is expensive to achieve and maintain. There are also "limited algorithms that are efficient for quantum parallelism, reducing the applicability of this method" (Tencent Cloud, 2025), meaning some types of PIC simulations may not be compatible with quantum parallelism methods.

To compare costs, a modern quantum computer, which is required to run quantum parallelism, "will range from $5 million to over $15 million" (Fit Design, 2025). In comparison, the components for parallel processing will be more affordable altogether, even with multiple components being required. For example, a high performance graphics card like the RTX 4090 "starts at £1499" (NVIDIA, 2025), which is a fraction of the price of a quantum system. As a result, building a parallel system requires less money, and therefore will be a much cheaper and more accessible way to compute PIC simulations. Aside from hardware, there will be additional costs produced from running the methods, with quantum computers being more expensive as they rely on specialised cooling systems that consume significantly large amounts of power. Additionally, quantum parallelism has more limited software in comparison to parallel processing, meaning specialised software would be required, reducing flexibility and restricting the amount of models that can be run.

Conclusion:
Taking all the points discussed into account, it is evident that parallel processing is the most applicable and cost-effective method for Particle-in-Cell simulations. It can process large volumes of data at high speeds, producing real-time results, unlike sequential processing. It also remains the most affordable and efficient method, enabling it to be accessible for the average researcher. Overall, parallel processing provides a practical approach for performing Particle-in-Cell simulations.
